Her Kind (poem)

From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
Jump to navigation Jump to search

"Her Kind" is a poem published in 1960 by American poet Anne Sexton. The poem describes a witch in the night; then describes her as an unconventional housewife; and concludes with reference to Carl Dryer's The Passion of Joan of Arc.[1][2] The American tendency to compartmentalize female identity into separate roles may be reflected in the clear, even division of stanzas, but ultimately, as noted by Claire Pollard, "all three roles infect the other surrounding them."[2] The imagery of the witch locates the speaker outside the bounds of social acceptance and highlights themes of female alienation in the poem.[3] While practicing witchcraft can be analyzed as the antithesis of motherhood,[4] Sexton employs the metaphor of "warm caves" in line eight to center the text within a gendered, unconventional, and mystical setting to further juxtapose the roles of motherhood against the social discriminations towards witchcraft.[3] "Her Kind" is divided into three seven-line stanzas with the refrain, "I have been her kind" every seventh line. The refrain parodies popular advertising techniques of the 1960s, specifically capitalist "This Is Your Wife" campaigns that reduced women to mere performers of a multitude of perfected social roles.[2]

"Her Kind" was published in Anne Sexton's first book, To Bedlam and Part Way Back against the advice of her mentor, John Holmes (poet). He warned her of later regretting her transparent and confessional style. Sexton understood this advice to be born from censorship, and cheekily dedicated the first poem of the book's second section to him ("To John, Who Begs Me Not to Enquire Further"), after composing a letter she never sent to him.[2]

"Her Kind" was always the first poem read by Sexton at public readings, and Sexton self-admitted that she derived an "orgasmic" pleasure from performance.[2][1] Sexton also once referred to her performance personality as a "little bit of a ham," and attended her readings only in fashionable outfits that bordered on the theatrical. She always had her nails polished and painted--a standard she had maintained since her modeling career.[1] It has been noted that Anne's own performance of femininity was inspired by her distant mother, Mary Gray Harvey. In her life, Harvey was celebrated for her unusually composed feminine demeanor and grand intellectual presence, only the latter she passed on to Anne.[5]

Context[edit]

Writing throughout the Cold War, Sexton was keenly aware of the economic importance of American housewives in the 1960s. The Kitchen Debate at the Sokolniki Exhibition and Convention Centre glorified middle-class American life and politicized the role of the housewife in the institution of capitalism. Nixon, pointing to a panel-controlled washing machine, said: "In America, these things are designed to make things easier for our women.”[2] Just as house-ware appliances were being newly engineered for ease and efficiency, so too was Nuclear warfare being designed for swiftness. The constant threat of nuclear warfare, especially following the global traumas of The Holocaust, augmented global consternation and fear for mass assured destruction as the possibility loomed for decades.[6] In turn, subdued and taboo topics such as suicide and mental illness were relished by American confessional poets as they rejected romanticism and modernism for a therapeutic, first-person speaker.[7] "Her Kind" concludes with an "[un]ashamed" (20) confession of suicide-desire that individualizes death before the twentieth-century backdrop of genocide and survival anxiety.[6]

Themes[edit]

Motherhood during the Cold War[edit]

Sexton's own disillusionment with motherhood is portrayed in her speaker's self-depiction as a witch who tends to the "worms and the elves" (11).[8] The choice of worms and elves dehumanizes the presence of children and is an unusual and socially controversial substitution. Instead of human children, worms and elves insinuates a certain perversity within the family unit, beginning with the witch mother.[2] As homemakers, the 1960s American housewife was expected to perform the duty of motherhood, and her failure to perform maternal care would result in disapproval and shaming from her suburban peers.[9] Sexton's contemporary, Adrienne Rich, theorized that new mothers eventually face a crisis in which their identity is stripped away against societal evaluations of a woman's worth, in relation to the duty of motherhood. Once born, the child's well-being takes precedence over the mother and becomes the woman's primary responsibility and only purpose.[4] Sexton, however, stated that instead of children, it was writing which "gave [her] a feeling of purpose, a little cause." After entering postpartum depression following the birth of her first child, Linda Gray Sexton, Anne was hospitalized for the first time in 1954.[5] As Sexton was far from the American ideal of motherhood, her husband Alfred "Kayo" Sexton II recounted how Anne's extraordinary anxieties would arise when asked to perform maternal tasks such as making appointments for the children or purchasing their clothes. When asked by Kayo to place a batch of potatoes in the oven, Anne admitted that she was unable to do so. Since the 1950s, the Cult of Domesticity had dominated American life, and this institution was directly challenged by "Her Kind" through the poem's self-identification within a broader history of female oppression, such as the trials of witches and Joan of Arc. Especially following the Kitchen Debate, the role and performance of motherhood was highly politicized.[4] As Robert Warshaw noted in 1948, "America, as a social and political organization, is committed to a cheerful view of life. If an American or Russian is unhappy, it implies a certain reprobation of his society.” In turn, Anne Sexton's extreme incompatibility and dissatisfaction with motherhood as depicted in "Her Kind" was socially considered to be a poor reflection upon both her family and the United States.[2]

Witches and Communism[edit]

In an interview with Gregory Fitz Gerald, Sexton joked, "the Puritans would have burned me at the stake, of course."[10] In "Her Kind," Sexton willfully identifies herself as a witch when she describes the various forms of social rebellion she has performed. In the first stanza, the witches' movement from the private sphere to the public sphere uses threatening diction, such as "haunting" (2), "dreaming evil," and "done my hitch" (3) to portray sexist and capitalist fears of women in the public arena.[2]

While the Cult of Domesticity located American women inside the home; conversely, under communism, women were encouraged to work in the public sphere.[9] The sinister language used in the first stanza aligns the witches' physical agency with the then-American paranoia of communism,[2] and her voyage outward to the "warm caves" subverts conventions of motherhood[7] in an animalistic fashion. However, the witch does still perform the domestic duties of a housewife as Sexton illuminates the mental consequences of automatically socializing girls and women into only maids, cooks, mothers, and homemakers.[5] The third stanza references Joan of Arc, the French icon who was burned at the stake in 1431 for the witch-like charge of summoning demons, but was later declared a saint in 1920.[11] Sexton's reference diffuses the significance of patriarchal labels as it is a reminder that the socially established distinction between a "witch" and a "saint" is both subjective and ambiguous.[3] In the second stanza, the witch attempts to perform domestic life inside the cave by filling it with common household objects and cooking dinner, but her consumerist approach at maternity is ultimately void of personal significance or meaning beyond her obligation to perform motherhood.[5] This section of the poem reflects the context of America's rising GDP from 1945-1960[7] because women then began to substitute their fragmented female identities with concrete commercial products in the consumerist craze. Anne Sexton even spent her Guggenheim grant money on a swimming pool as an outward, performative assertion of her successful middle-class American life. In a letter to her mother, Sexton wrote "my heart's desire is an electric mix-master with the orange juice squeezer on top." Sexton's covert sarcasm is both an extension and a critique of the advertisement-crazed America as she experienced it.[2] In an interview with Patricia Marx, Sexton referred to the "brainwashing pablum of advertisements every minute"[12] that she subtly mocks with the refrain in "Her Kind."[2]

Criticism[edit]

To Bedlam and Part Way Back received criticism on the basis of relying too heavily on biographical events that crossed the poetic boundaries of what was deemed acceptably vulnerable. Even though Sexton's confessional poetry is not solely rooted in fact,[12] critics like Patricia Meyer Spacks asked, "how can the reader properly respond to lines as grotesquely uncontrolled as these?" Likewise, James Dickey found himself negatively unable to interact with Sexton's confessional style because it was too transparently autobiographical.[13] As a confessional poem, "Her Kind" transposes the poet's identity with the text's speaker[6] through a strong, declarative tone that is typically utilized in American confessional poetry.[1][7] Sexton's "Her Kind" is a clear departure from what she described as "over-literary, over-intellectualized" early post-war poetry.[7][5][14] Being a pioneer of American confessional poetry, the most common complaint against Sexton's work, and especially so inTo Bedlam and Part Way Back, is a perceived over-indulgence of the self.[2][7][13][15] John Holmes refuted her work on a fundamental level, stating that "her motives are wrong artistically."[5] However, other critics such as Robert Phillips and Laurence Lerner contend that the often exposed biographical content of confessional poetry displays bravery on the author's behalf.[13] Despite the seemingly personal vulnerability of confessional poetry, Sexton also utilized personas in her poetry and altered facts of her life to reach into the emotional marrow of her text.[2][12]
